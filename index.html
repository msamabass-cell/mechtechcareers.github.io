The Journey

From factory floor to your future

This career path is not just a résumé — it is a map of the industry. Understanding where the journey has been helps you see exactly where MechTech can take you.

The journey began designing plastic and sheet metal components for Volvo and Mack trucks at Volvo Eicher Commercial Vehicles — working on cost reduction programs, lightweight projects, and interior trim development. That chapter also included working onsite at Volvo Greensboro, USA, providing rare first-hand exposure to how global engineering teams actually operate.

At Force Motors Ltd., the work expanded to leading the full GA layout for Project T1N (Urbania), developing modular seating systems, designing ambulance variants to government specifications, and managing cross-functional teams from concept to production sign-off — over a decade of designing, iterating, and delivering real products to market.

While pursuing a Master's at the University of North Texas, simultaneously working on a NASA MUREP-funded project opened a new world: federally funded research, international academic networks, conference presentations, and co-authored publications. That experience is the foundation for guiding MechTech students toward funded research, overseas study, and global careers.

Across all of it, the same pattern emerged: a massive gap between what engineering colleges produce and what industry — and the world — needs. MechTech Careers is the answer to that gap.

"I didn't build MechTech because it seemed like a good business. I built it because I've sat in every chair — the design chair, the manager's chair, the researcher's chair — and I know exactly what's missing when fresh engineers walk in on day one."

— Sameer Abass, Founder

The Gap MechTech Closes

What college doesn't prepare you for

Years and years inside industry made one thing unmistakably clear — the exact skills that separate hired engineers from overlooked ones. MechTech is built around closing every one of them.

❌ What Most Graduates Lack

Industry-standard CAD workflowsCollege teaches modelling. Industry expects DFM-aware design, master sections, B-side features, and tooling feasibility checks.
Working knowledge of GD&TMost graduates can define it. Very few can apply tolerances under real production constraints.
Product lifecycle understandingColleges teach design. They rarely teach proto builds, fitment trials, and sign-off procedures.
PLM & documentation skillsENOVIA, Windchill, SOR preparation — standard in OEMs, almost never taught in college.
Global career awarenessMost graduates don't know how to position themselves for overseas roles, funded research, or international MS programs.

✅ What MechTech Builds in You
